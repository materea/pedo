    Introduction

    Snakes are fascinating creatures, with more than 3000 known species known to humans. However, out of this vast number, only around 200 are considered poisonous to humans. Understanding these reptiles and their behaviors is essential, especially when it comes to the prevention and treatment of snakebites.

    Snakebites most often occur when humans encroach upon the snake's natural habitat. These habitats can vary widely and may include bodies of water, underground holes, tree stumps, or even refuse sites.

    It's important to note that most snakebites occur as a defensive response by the snake. When threatened or cornered, snakes may strike as a means of self-preservation. Therefore, knowing how to coexist with these creatures and respond to snakebites is crucial for both human safety and the conservation of these remarkable animals.

    Poisonous Snakes

    There are several families of poisonous snakes, each with its own unique characteristics. Here are some of the key families of poisonous snakes:

    • Elapidae: This family includes snakes like:
      • Cobras
      • Mambas
      • Kraits
      • Coral snakes
      These snakes are known for their potent venom.
    • Hydrophidae: Hydrophidae is the family of sea snakes. These snakes are adapted to marine environments and can be found in the ocean.
    • Viperidae: The Viperidae family includes snakes like:
      • Puff adder
      • Russell's viper
      These snakes are known for their distinctively shaped heads and long fangs.
    • Viperidae (Crotalidae): This subfamily includes snakes like:
      • Rattlesnakes
      • Cottonmouths
      • Copperheads
      They are often referred to as pit vipers due to the heat-sensing pits located between their eyes and nostrils.
    • Colubridae: While many members of the Colubridae family are non-venomous, some, like the Boomslang, are venomous. It's important to note that not all Colubridae snakes are dangerous.

    It's worth mentioning that approximately 90% of poisonous snakes belong to one of these families. Understanding the characteristics and distribution of these families is essential for identifying and treating snakebites effectively.

    Pit Vipers

    • Pit Vipers are a subfamily of Viperidae known for distinctive characteristics:
      • Triangular head
      • Pits on the sides of the head
      • Ellipse-shaped eyes
      • Extremely poisonous
      • Often found in the USA

    True Vipers

    • True Vipers are characterized by the following features:
      • Triangular head
      • Absent pits
      • Ellipse-shaped eyes
      • Extremely poisonous
      • Often found in Southern parts of Nigeria
      • Prototype member is Echis carinatus

    Elapids

    • Elapids exhibit the following distinctive traits:
      • Broad neck
      • Short hollow fangs
      • Head covered with scales
      • Usually indistinct neck
      • Most members are venomous
      • Cobras are commonly found in Northern Nigeria

    Hydrophidae

    • Hydrophidae is characterized by its aquatic existence and unique features:
      • Aquatic existence
      • Paddle-like tail
      • Small eyes with rounded pupils
      • Relatively uncommon in Africa
      • Prototype members include pelagic seasnake and sea krait

    Colubridae

    • Colubridae is a diverse family with varying characteristics:
      • Most members are nonvenomous
      • Have elongated grooved teeth at the back of the mouth
      • Boomslangs inflate their heads when threatened

    Epidemiology of Snake Bites

    ×

    • The incidence of snakebites has been reported as 497 per 100,000 population per year.
    • The estimated mortality rate is 12 percent.
    • Echis carinatus (carpet viper) accounts for at least 66 percent of snakebites in certain regions.
    • Snakebites often occur while victims are engaged in activities such as farming, herding, or walking. However, the spitting cobra may bite victims who inadvertently roll upon it in their sleep.
    • Bites are more common in male children.

    Pathogenesis

    • Snake venom composition: Snake venom is a complex mixture of various components, including proteases, proteins, polysaccharides, glycoproteins, and several vasoactive substances.
    • Diverse effects: Snake venom results in diverse effects that affect multiple systems in the body.
    • Classification of snake venoms: Snake venoms may be broadly classified based on their predominant action into the following categories:
      • Cytolytic
      • Haematotoxic
      • Myotoxic
      • Neurotoxic
    • Elapidae venom: Venom from the Elapidae family is primarily neurotoxic. It has a curare-like effect and acts by blocking neurotransmission at the neuromuscular junction.
    • Hydrophidae venom: Venom from the Hydrophidae family is myotoxic, causing muscle cell injury. It also has neurotoxic properties.
    • Viperidae venom: Venom from the Viperidae family is primarily cytolytic and haemotoxic. It often causes tissue necrosis and damages blood vessels, leading to vascular leakage and subsequent oedema. Damage to vascular endothelium can stimulate excessive micro-intravascular clotting, resulting in consumptive coagulopathy.

    Clinical Features

    • Anxiety: Anxiety is the most common initial feature of snakebite. It often leads to unnecessary and potentially harmful actions.
    • Local features: Local effects at the snakebite site include:
      • Pain and swelling at the site
      • Bleeding
      • Erythema (redness)
      • Tingling sensation at the site
    • Systemic features: Snakebite can lead to various systemic effects, including:
      • Headache
      • Vomiting
      • Epistaxis (nosebleeds)
      • Gastrointestinal bleeding
      • Diaphoresis (excessive sweating)
      • Ptosis (drooping of eyelids)
      • Paralysis of limbs
      • Drowsiness
      • Loss of muscle coordination
      • Abdominal pains
      • Tachypnoea (rapid breathing)
      • Tachycardia (rapid heart rate)
      • Passage of dark-colored urine
      • Acute renal failure

    Assessment

    • Age of child: Assess the age of the child affected by the snakebite.
    • Site of bite: Determine the specific location on the body where the snakebite occurred.
    • Circumstances surrounding bites: Consider the circumstances or events leading up to the snakebite.
    • Secondary bites: Investigate the possibility of multiple snakebites.
    • Identification of snake: If possible, try to identify the snake species responsible for the bite.
    • Care given thus far: Inquire about any initial care or treatment provided to the victim.
    • Inspect for fang marks: Examine the bite site for the presence of fang marks or puncture wounds.
    • Demarcate area of swelling: Mark the boundaries of swelling or local reactions caused by the snakebite.

    General Symptoms

    • Cardiovascular (Viperidae): Symptoms include visual disturbances, dizziness, faintness, collapse, shock, hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias, and pulmonary edema.
    • Bleeding and clotting disorders (Viperidae): This category includes various bleeding and clotting symptoms such as:
      • Bleeding from recent wounds (including fang marks and venepunctures) and from old partly-healed wounds
      • Spontaneous systemic bleeding, which can manifest as bleeding from gums, epistaxis, bleeding into tears, haemoptysis, haematemesis, rectal bleeding or melaena, haematuria, vaginal bleeding, bleeding into the skin (petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses), and mucosae (e.g., conjunctivae)
      • Intracranial haemorrhage
    • Neurological (Elapidae, Russell‚Äôs viper): Neurological symptoms may include drowsiness, paraesthesiae, abnormalities of taste and smell, "heavy" eyelids, ptosis, external ophthalmoplegia, paralysis of facial muscles and other muscles innervated by cranial nerves, aphonia, difficulty in swallowing secretions, respiratory and generalized flaccid paralysis.
    • Skeletal muscle breakdown (sea snakes, Russell‚Äôs viper): Symptoms include generalized pain, stiffness, and tenderness of muscles, trismus, myoglobinuria, hyperkalaemia, cardiac arrest, and acute renal failure.
    • Renal (Viperidae, sea snakes): Renal symptoms may include loin (lower back) pain, haematuria, haemoglobinuria, myoglobinuria, oliguria/anuria, and symptoms and signs of uraemia (acidotic breathing, hiccups, nausea, pleuritic chest pain, etc.).
    • Endocrine (acute pituitary/adrenal insufficiency) (Russell‚Äôs viper): Endocrine-related symptoms may manifest as follows:
      • Acute phase: shock, hypoglycaemia
      • Chronic phase (months to years after the bite): weakness, loss of secondary sexual hair, amenorrhoea, testicular atrophy, hypothyroidism, etc.

    Classification of Envenomation

    • Envenomation can be classified into four grades:
      • Grade 0: No envenomation
      • Grade 1: Minimal envenomation, characterized by local swelling and pain without progression
      • Grade 2: Moderate envenomation, which includes swelling, pain, or ecchymosis that progresses beyond the site of injury, along with mild systemic or laboratory manifestations
      • Grade 3: Severe envenomation, marked by a significant local response, severe systemic findings, and substantial alterations in laboratory findings

    Investigations

    • FBC (Full Blood Count): assesses the levels of various blood components, including red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.
    • Platelet count: Measures the number of platelets in the blood, which is important for blood clotting. The value can is decreased when DIC complicates snakebite.
    • E/U/Cr (Electrolytes/Urea/Creatinine): checks the levels of electrolytes (sodium, potassium, etc.), urea, and creatinine in the blood, which helps assess kidney function and electrolyte balance.
    • LFT (Liver Function Tests): Evaluates the function of the liver by measuring various enzymes and proteins in the blood.
    • GXM (GXM Blood Group and Crossmatch): determines the patient's blood group and checks for compatibility in case a blood transfusion is needed.
    • Bedside clotting time: Assesses the time it takes for blood to clot, which can indicate clotting abnormalities.
    • PT (Prothrombin Time) / PTTK (Partial Thromboplastin Time): These tests measure the time it takes for blood to clot and help assess the functioning of clotting factors in the blood.

    Treatment

    • At site care: Initial care provided at the site of the snakebite to minimize its effects and prevent infection. It includes tying a torniquet proximal to the bite such that two fingers can still enter the torniquet, the aim is to block the lymphatics, not the veins or arteries.
    • Mechanism of hospital transportation: Details on how the patient should be safely transported to the hospital for further treatment.
    • Anti-snake venom: Administration of specific anti-snake venom to counteract the effects of snake venom. Antivenoms should be given within 24 hours, preferably within 4 to 6 hours of the bite as it shows little or no benefit after 24 hours. Anti-snake venoms can be monovalent (for a single type of snake) or polyvalent (for multiple types of snakes, has a higher risk of anaphylaxis.)
    • Antibiotics: Use of antibiotics to prevent or treat secondary infections resulting from the snakebite e.g. penicillin, metronidazole.
    • Fluid therapy: Give One and a half maintenance fluid if bite is myotoxic.
    • Assisted respiration: A ventilator can be used in neurotoxic bites.
    • Prevention of tetanus: Measures taken to prevent tetanus infection, which can occur as a complication of snakebite e.g. proper wound dressing.
    • Management of complications: Strategies for addressing any complications that may arise due to the snakebite or its treatment.
